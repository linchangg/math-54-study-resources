Lecture 32 · B4.6 & B9.1
VOP with Nonconstant Coefficients & Systems Intro

This lecture has two distinct parts. The first revisits Variation of Parameters (VOP) and asks: does it still work when the coefficients of the ODE are functions of \(t\) rather than constants? The answer is yes — and it's remarkably elegant. The second part opens up an entirely new chapter: what does it even mean to have a system of differential equations, and how do we write one down cleanly?

Part 1 — VOP Extended
Does VOP still work when the coefficients aren't constants?

Up until now, whenever we solved a nonhomogeneous equation like \(ay'' + by' + cy = f(t)\), the leading coefficient \(a\) was always a plain number. Lecture 32 asks: what if it's a function of \(t\)? What if we have something like \(ty'' - (t+1)y' + y = t^2\), where the coefficient in front of \(y''\) is \(t\) itself?

The good news is that VOP doesn't care. When we derived the VOP formulas for \(u_1'\) and \(u_2'\), we never actually used the fact that \(a, b, c\) were constants — only that we had two known homogeneous solutions \(y_1\) and \(y_2\). So the same formulas apply word-for-word. The only difference: instead of dividing \(f(t)\) by the constant \(a\), you divide by the function \(a(t)\).

VOP formulas — work for both constant and nonconstant \(a(t)\)
\[ u_1' = \frac{-y_2 \cdot \frac{f(t)}{a(t)}}{W(y_1,y_2)}, \qquad u_2' = \frac{y_1 \cdot \frac{f(t)}{a(t)}}{W(y_1,y_2)} \]
where \(W(y_1,y_2) = y_1 y_2' - y_2 y_1'\) is the Wronskian, and \(a(t)\) is whatever the leading coefficient is — constant or not.
⚠️
But there's a catch. To use these formulas, you first need \(y_1\) and \(y_2\) — the two linearly independent solutions to the complementary (homogeneous) equation. With constant coefficients, finding them was easy: use the characteristic equation and you're done. With nonconstant coefficients, there's no characteristic equation. You can't just write down the solutions. They might come from a Taylor series, from a clever substitution, or from being told — as in most exam problems. This is the real difficulty, not the VOP step itself.

So in a sense, the hard problem with nonconstant coefficients is finding \(y_1, y_2\) in the first place. Once you have them, getting from the homogeneous solution to the particular solution is no harder than before.

💡
Why not just always use VOP then? A reasonable question — if VOP handles both constant and nonconstant coefficients, why did we bother learning Undetermined Coefficients (MUC) at all? The answer: MUC is usually much faster when it applies (constant coefficients + right kind of \(f(t)\)). VOP always works but always requires integration, which can be painful. Both tools have their place.
worked example · B4.6.23
Worked Example — VOP with nonconstant coefficients
Finding a particular solution when \(y_1, y_2\) are given

Consider the equation \(ty'' - (t+1)y' + y = t^2\), where we are told that \(y_1 = e^t\) and \(y_2 = t+1\) are the two homogeneous solutions. Our job is to find a particular solution \(y_p\) using VOP.

Steps Full solution walkthrough click to collapse ▾
1
Put the equation in standard form first
The VOP formulas require the equation to be in the form \(y'' + P(t)y' + Q(t)y = g(t)\), meaning the leading coefficient must be 1. Our equation has leading coefficient \(t\), so divide everything through by \(t\):
\[ y'' - \frac{t+1}{t}\,y' + \frac{1}{t}\,y = t \]
Now we can identify \(g(t) = t\) — this is the right-hand side after dividing. (Note: the original \(f(t) = t^2\) divided by \(a(t) = t\) gives \(g(t) = t\).)
2
Compute the Wronskian \(W(y_1, y_2)\)
The Wronskian is the determinant of the matrix formed by \(y_1, y_2\) and their derivatives. It appears in the denominator of both VOP formulas, so we compute it first:
\[ W = \begin{vmatrix} y_1 & y_2 \\ y_1' & y_2' \end{vmatrix} = \begin{vmatrix} e^t & t+1 \\ e^t & 1 \end{vmatrix} = e^t \cdot 1 - (t+1) \cdot e^t = e^t(1 - t - 1) = -te^t \]
So \(W = -te^t\). Note it's nonzero (for \(t \neq 0\)), confirming \(y_1\) and \(y_2\) are linearly independent — as they should be.
3
Find \(u_1'\) using the VOP formula
The formula is \(u_1' = \frac{-y_2 \cdot g(t)}{W}\). Plug in \(y_2 = t+1\), \(g(t) = t\), and \(W = -te^t\):
\[ u_1' = \frac{-(t+1) \cdot t}{-te^t} = \frac{t(t+1)}{te^t} = \frac{t+1}{e^t} = (t+1)e^{-t} \]
The two negatives cancel, giving a positive result. Now we integrate to find \(u_1\):
\[ u_1 = \int (t+1)e^{-t}\,dt \]
This requires integration by parts. Let \(u = (t+1)\), \(dv = e^{-t}\,dt\), so \(du = dt\) and \(v = -e^{-t}\):
\[ u_1 = -(t+1)e^{-t} + \int e^{-t}\,dt = -(t+1)e^{-t} - e^{-t} = -(t+2)e^{-t} \]
4
Find \(u_2'\) using the VOP formula
The formula is \(u_2' = \frac{y_1 \cdot g(t)}{W}\). Plug in \(y_1 = e^t\), \(g(t) = t\), \(W = -te^t\):
\[ u_2' = \frac{e^t \cdot t}{-te^t} = \frac{te^t}{-te^t} = -1 \]
Remarkably clean — \(e^t\) and \(t\) both cancel. Integrating gives:
\[ u_2 = \int -1\,dt = -t \]
5
Assemble \(y_p = u_1 y_1 + u_2 y_2\)
Now multiply each \(u\) by its matching homogeneous solution and add:
\[ y_p = u_1 y_1 + u_2 y_2 = \underbrace{-(t+2)e^{-t}}_{u_1} \cdot \underbrace{e^t}_{y_1} + \underbrace{(-t)}_{u_2} \cdot \underbrace{(t+1)}_{y_2} \]
The first term simplifies beautifully: \(e^{-t} \cdot e^t = 1\), so \(-(t+2)e^{-t} \cdot e^t = -(t+2)\). The second term expands to \(-t(t+1) = -t^2 - t\). Combining:
\[ y_p = -(t+2) + (-t^2 - t) = -(t^2 + 2t + 2) \]
6
Write the general solution
The general solution is the homogeneous solution plus the particular solution:
General solution
\[ y(t) = c_1 e^t + c_2(t+1) - (t^2 + 2t + 2) \]
The first two terms are \(y_c\) — the complementary solution with arbitrary constants \(c_1, c_2\). The last term is \(y_p\), a specific polynomial that accounts for the \(t^2\) forcing on the right side. Initial conditions, if given, would determine \(c_1\) and \(c_2\).
part 2 — chapter B9: systems of differential equations
Part 2 — Chapter B9 Introduction
What is a system of differential equations?

Up until now we've always had a single unknown function — just \(y(t)\). A system of differential equations involves multiple unknown functions of the same variable, all coupled together. For instance, \(x_1(t)\) and \(x_2(t)\) might both depend on \(t\), and the rate of change of each one might depend on the current values of both.

A classic example from Calculus is the predator-prey model: let \(x_1\) be the prey population and \(x_2\) be the predator population. Both change over time, and each population's rate of change depends on how many of the other exist right now:

Predator-prey (Lotka-Volterra) — a nonlinear system
\[\begin{aligned} x_1' &= 2x_1 - 5x_1 x_2 \\ x_2' &= -3x_2 + 4x_1 x_2 \end{aligned}\]
Prey (\(x_1\)) grows on its own (the \(+2x_1\) term) but is eaten by predators (the \(-5x_1 x_2\) term). Predators (\(x_2\)) die off on their own (the \(-3x_2\) term) but are fed by eating prey (the \(+4x_1 x_2\) term).
✗
Why can't we solve this with matrices? The predator-prey system contains \(x_1 x_2\) terms — the product of two unknowns. That makes it nonlinear. Linear algebra requires linear equations, and \(x_1 x_2\) is quadratic. If we tried an exponential solution \(x_j = c_j e^{kt}\), then \(x_1 x_2 = c_1 c_2 e^{2kt}\) — a different exponential rate. The exponentials on the two sides of the equation would never match up, so the method fails completely.

What we can solve are linear systems — where each equation is a linear combination of the unknowns, with no products like \(x_1 x_2\). Mixing problems (two tanks feeding each other salt) are a natural source of linear systems, because the concentration of salt in a pipe is proportional to the amount in the source tank — no squaring or multiplication of unknowns.

Part 3 — The Matrix Form
Writing a linear system as \(\mathbf{x}' = A\mathbf{x}\)

A linear system of differential equations looks like a bunch of equations where each \(x_j'\) equals some linear combination of all the \(x\)'s. The elegant observation is that we can package all of this into a single matrix equation. Stack the unknown functions into a column vector \(\mathbf{x}(t)\), read the coefficients into a matrix \(A\), and the whole system becomes:

General linear system in matrix form
\[ \mathbf{x}'(t) = A(t)\,\mathbf{x}(t) + \mathbf{f}(t) \]
where \(\mathbf{x}(t)\) is \(n \times 1\), \(A(t)\) is \(n \times n\), and \(\mathbf{f}(t)\) is \(n \times 1\). When \(\mathbf{f}(t) = \mathbf{0}\), the system is called homogeneous. When \(A\) has only constant entries, it has constant coefficients.

Concretely: if we have the system \(x_1' = -4x_1 + x_2\) and \(x_2' = 3x_1 - 2x_2\), we just read off the coefficients row by row into the matrix \(A\), exactly as we did when converting systems of linear algebraic equations to matrix form earlier in the course:

Example: reading off the coefficient matrix
\[ \begin{bmatrix}x_1'\\x_2'\end{bmatrix} = \underbrace{\begin{bmatrix}-4 & 1 \\ 3 & -2\end{bmatrix}}_{A}\begin{bmatrix}x_1\\x_2\end{bmatrix} \]
✓
Why constant coefficients matter so much. If \(A\) has only constants, then the entries of \(A\) don't change over time, and the system has a clean exponential character. We can hope that \(\mathbf{x}(t) = e^{\lambda t}\mathbf{v}\) for some \(\lambda\) and \(\mathbf{v}\) — this will be the entire basis of Lectures 33 and 34. If \(A\) has functions of \(t\) in it, that approach breaks down and we're mostly stuck.
Higher-order ODEs are secretly just systems

There's a subtle but important connection: any single higher-order ODE can be rewritten as a system of first-order equations. For example, take \(ty''' + t^2 y'' + y' + 5y = 0\). Define \(x_1 = y\), \(x_2 = y'\), \(x_3 = y''\). Then \(x_1' = x_2\), \(x_2' = x_3\), and \(x_3' = y'''\), which we can solve for from the original equation. The whole thing becomes:

A 3rd-order ODE written as a 3×3 system
\[ \begin{bmatrix}x_1\\x_2\\x_3\end{bmatrix}' = \begin{bmatrix}0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \\ -5/t & -1/t & -t\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}x_1\\x_2\\x_3\end{bmatrix} \]
This particular matrix has \(t\)-dependent entries, so we can't solve it using the eigenvalue methods coming up. But a constant-coefficient second-order ODE \(ay'' + by' + cy = 0\) would give a constant matrix with entries \(0, 1, -c/a, -b/a\) — and that we can handle.
The key takeaway from this half of the lecture: writing a system in matrix form doesn't automatically make it easier to solve. The real power comes when the matrix \(A\) has constant entries — because then the whole eigenvalue machinery of linear algebra becomes available to us. That's what the rest of the chapter is about.
Part 4 — Scope of the Chapter
What kinds of systems will we actually solve?

Not all systems are equal. The lecture gives a clear map of what's accessible:

✓ Homogeneous, constant coefficients
\(\mathbf{x}' = A\mathbf{x}\) where \(A\) is a constant matrix. This is the main case. Solutions come from eigenvalues and eigenvectors of \(A\). Covered in B9.5 (Lec 33) and B9.6 (Lec 34).
~ Nonhomogeneous, constant coefficients
\(\mathbf{x}' = A\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{f}(t)\) where \(A\) is constant but \(\mathbf{f}(t) \neq 0\). Harder, but if \(\mathbf{f}\) is a simple constant (like in the salt-replenishment scenario), sometimes still doable.
✗ Nonconstant coefficients
If entries of \(A\) contain \(t\), eigenvalue methods fail. Volume-in-tank changing over time is one example — the concentrations get \(t\) in the denominator. Not accessible to us.
✗ Nonlinear systems
Predator-prey type systems with \(x_1 x_2\) terms. Matrix methods don't apply at all. Beyond the scope of this course entirely.

The mixing problem (two tanks, constant volumes, constant flow rates) gives a homogeneous constant-coefficient system — which is exactly the case we can handle. Replenishing with saltwater adds a constant \(\mathbf{f}(t)\), making it nonhomogeneous. Changing volumes put \(t\) in the matrix entries, making it nonconstant. The lecture points these out not to solve them, but to understand why the nice case is nice and why the other cases are harder.

Lecture 33 · B9.5
Real Eigenvalues & Mixing Problems

This lecture answers the central question of Chapter B9: how do you actually solve the system \(\mathbf{x}' = A\mathbf{x}\)? The answer turns out to be surprisingly elegant — it comes directly from the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of \(A\).

Part 1 — The Core Idea
Where do we even start?

Back when we had a single equation like \(x' = ax\), the solution was simply \(x(t) = Ce^{at}\) — an exponential. When we move to a system of equations \(\mathbf{x}' = A\mathbf{x}\), where \(\mathbf{x}(t)\) is now a whole vector of functions, it's natural to ask: can solutions still be exponential in some sense?

The key insight is to guess that every component of \(\mathbf{x}(t)\) shares the same exponential rate. That is, try:

The educated guess (ansatz)
\[ \mathbf{x}(t) = e^{\lambda t}\,\mathbf{w} \quad\text{where}\quad \mathbf{w} = \begin{bmatrix}w_1 \\ w_2 \\ \vdots \\ w_n\end{bmatrix} \text{ is a constant vector} \]

Here, \(e^{\lambda t}\) is the shared exponential growth/decay rate, and \(\mathbf{w}\) is a constant vector that describes the shape or direction of the solution — how the components of \(\mathbf{x}\) relate to each other. The reason this guess makes sense: if we plug it in, every term on both sides will be proportional to \(e^{\lambda t}\), so there's actually a chance the equation can balance.

💡
Why the same exponent for every component? In the equation \(\mathbf{x}' = A\mathbf{x}\), the matrix \(A\) mixes all components together. If different components grew at different rates, the ratios between them would change over time — and multiplying by the constant matrix \(A\) can't produce that. A consistent exponential rate is the only hope.
Part 2 — Why It Works
The verification: eigenvalues appear naturally

Let's plug the guess \(\mathbf{x}(t) = Ce^{\lambda t}\mathbf{w}\) into the equation \(\mathbf{x}' = A\mathbf{x}\) and see what conditions we need. We compute both sides separately:

Left side — differentiate \(\mathbf{x}(t)\)
Since \(C\) and \(\mathbf{w}\) are constants (no \(t\)), differentiating just brings down \(\lambda\) from the exponent:
\[ \mathbf{x}'(t) = C\lambda\, e^{\lambda t}\,\mathbf{w} \]
Right side — apply \(A\) to \(\mathbf{x}(t)\)
Since \(A\) is a constant matrix, we can pull the scalar \(Ce^{\lambda t}\) out front:
\[ A\mathbf{x}(t) = A\!\left[Ce^{\lambda t}\mathbf{w}\right] = Ce^{\lambda t}\!\left[A\mathbf{w}\right] \]

For the two sides to be equal, we need \(C\lambda\, e^{\lambda t}\,\mathbf{w} = Ce^{\lambda t}[A\mathbf{w}]\). Canceling \(Ce^{\lambda t}\) (which is never zero), this reduces to:

The condition for the guess to work
\[ A\mathbf{w} = \lambda\,\mathbf{w} \]

This is exactly the definition of an eigenvalue \(\lambda\) and eigenvector \(\mathbf{w}\) of the matrix \(A\). So the guess works if and only if \(\lambda\) is an eigenvalue of \(A\) and \(\mathbf{w}\) is a corresponding eigenvector. This is why eigenvectors, which might seem like a purely linear algebra concept, turn out to be the heart of solving differential equations.

✓
The punchline: For each eigenvalue \(\lambda\) of \(A\) with eigenvector \(\mathbf{w}\), the function \(\mathbf{x}(t) = Ce^{\lambda t}\mathbf{w}\) is a solution to \(\mathbf{x}' = A\mathbf{x}\). We keep the constant \(C\) out front so we can scale the solution freely — since any nonzero multiple of an eigenvector is still an eigenvector, \(C\) generalizes the family.
Part 3 — Putting It All Together
The general solution: one term per eigenvalue

An \(n \times n\) system \(\mathbf{x}' = A\mathbf{x}\) has \(n\) unknown functions, so — just like an \(n\)th-order single equation — we expect the general solution to involve \(n\) linearly independent pieces. As long as \(A\) is diagonalizable over the reals (meaning it has \(n\) linearly independent real eigenvectors), we get exactly those pieces from the eigenvalues:

General solution — diagonalizable case
\[ \mathbf{x}(t) = c_1\,e^{\lambda_1 t}\,\mathbf{v}_1 \;+\; c_2\,e^{\lambda_2 t}\,\mathbf{v}_2 \;+\; \cdots \;+\; c_n\,e^{\lambda_n t}\,\mathbf{v}_n \]
where \(\lambda_1,\ldots,\lambda_n\) are the eigenvalues and \(\mathbf{v}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{v}_n\) are corresponding linearly independent eigenvectors of \(A\).

The constants \(c_1, \ldots, c_n\) are determined by initial conditions. The matrix whose columns are the vectors \(e^{\lambda_j t}\mathbf{v}_j\) is called the fundamental matrix — it's a compact way of packaging all \(n\) solutions at once, analogous to how a basis packages a vector space.

What if \(A\) isn't diagonalizable?

Two things can prevent \(A\) from being diagonalizable over the reals:

Case 1 — Repeated real eigenvalue
If an eigenvalue appears multiple times but doesn't have enough independent eigenvectors to match, we're missing solutions. The fix is similar to single equations: the extra solutions involve a factor of \(t\), i.e. terms of the form \(te^{rt}\). Working this out for a 2×2 matrix is already quite involved (see HW B9.5.35); for larger matrices it gets much harder.
Case 2 — Nonreal (complex) eigenvalues
This is actually more tractable! When eigenvalues come in conjugate pairs \(\alpha \pm \beta i\), we use Euler's identity to split the complex solution into two real-valued solutions involving \(\cos\) and \(\sin\). This is the whole story of Lecture 34.

Worked Example
Solving a 2×2 system step by step

Consider the system:

The system
\[\begin{aligned} x_1' &= -4x_1 + 2x_2 \\ x_2' &= \phantom{-}2x_1 - x_2 \end{aligned}\]

The first step is always to read off the coefficient matrix \(A\) — the entries are just the coefficients of \(x_1\) and \(x_2\) on the right-hand side of each equation:

Steps Full solution walkthrough click to collapse ▾
1
Write the matrix \(A\)
The coefficient matrix is formed by reading off the coefficients row by row. Row 1 comes from the equation for \(x_1'\): its coefficient on \(x_1\) is \(-4\), and on \(x_2\) is \(+2\). Row 2 comes from the equation for \(x_2'\).
\[ A = \begin{bmatrix}-4 & 2 \\ 2 & -1\end{bmatrix} \]
2
Find the eigenvalues — set \(\det(A - \lambda I) = 0\)
We subtract \(\lambda\) from each diagonal entry and set the determinant to zero. This gives the characteristic polynomial:
\[ \det\!\begin{bmatrix}-4-\lambda & 2 \\ 2 & -1-\lambda\end{bmatrix} = (-4-\lambda)(-1-\lambda) - (2)(2) \]
Expanding: \((-4-\lambda)(-1-\lambda) = 4 + 4\lambda + \lambda + \lambda^2 = \lambda^2 + 5\lambda + 4\). Subtracting \(4\) from the cross term gives \(\lambda^2 + 5\lambda = \lambda(\lambda+5) = 0\).
\[ \lambda_1 = {\color{#c47b7b}{-5}}, \qquad \lambda_2 = {\color{#c47b7b}{0}} \]
Note that \(\lambda_2 = 0\) is a perfectly valid eigenvalue! A zero eigenvalue means the corresponding solution term is \(e^{0 \cdot t}\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{v}\) — a constant vector. This means the system has a mode that doesn't decay at all.
3
Find the eigenvector for \(\lambda_1 = -5\)
Plug \(\lambda = -5\) into \(A - \lambda I\) and row reduce. Adding 5 to the diagonal of \(A\):
\[ A + 5I = \begin{bmatrix}1 & 2 \\ 2 & 4\end{bmatrix} \sim \begin{bmatrix}1 & 2 \\ 0 & 0\end{bmatrix} \]
The single equation is \(w_1 + 2w_2 = 0\), so \(w_1 = -2w_2\). Choose \(w_2 = 1\) to get the simplest eigenvector:
\[ \mathbf{v}_1 = \begin{bmatrix}-2 \\ 1\end{bmatrix} \]
4
Find the eigenvector for \(\lambda_2 = 0\)
With \(\lambda = 0\), we just row reduce \(A\) itself:
\[ A = \begin{bmatrix}-4 & 2 \\ 2 & -1\end{bmatrix} \sim \begin{bmatrix}-4 & 2 \\ 0 & 0\end{bmatrix} \]
The equation is \(-4w_1 + 2w_2 = 0\), so \(w_2 = 2w_1\). Choosing \(w_1 = 1\):
\[ \mathbf{v}_2 = \begin{bmatrix}1 \\ 2\end{bmatrix} \]
5
Assemble the general solution
Each eigenvalue–eigenvector pair gives one solution term. We take a linear combination of all of them:
General solution
\[ \mathbf{x}(t) = c_1\, e^{-5t}\begin{bmatrix}-2\\1\end{bmatrix} + c_2\begin{bmatrix}1\\2\end{bmatrix} \]
Written as scalar components: \(x_1(t) = -2c_1 e^{-5t} + c_2\) and \(x_2(t) = c_1 e^{-5t} + 2c_2\). The second term is a constant (since \(\lambda_2 = 0\)), meaning if you start with a particular ratio between \(x_1\) and \(x_2\), that part of the state never changes — only the \(e^{-5t}\) part decays away.

Part 4 — Physical Application
Two tanks, salt, and a system of DEs

Here's a classic application: two tanks of brine (saltwater) connected by pipes. Salt flows between them over time. We want to track how much salt is in each tank at any moment. Let's see how this naturally becomes a linear system \(\mathbf{x}' = A\mathbf{x}\).

Why do volumes stay fixed? Before we can write any equations, we must verify that the 50 L in each tank is constant. Check: Tank A receives 3 L/min (pure water) + 1 L/min (from B) = 4 L/min total in, and sends out 4 L/min to B. Net change = 0. Tank B receives 4 L/min from A, and sends 1 L/min back + 3 L/min out = 4 L/min total out. Net change = 0. Both volumes stay fixed. This matters because otherwise the concentration denominators would have \(t\) in them, giving non-constant coefficients we can't easily handle.

Now we model each tank using the "rate in minus rate out" principle. The rate at which salt enters or leaves a tank through a pipe equals the concentration of salt in the source tank (kg per liter) times the flow rate (liters per minute). Concentration = (kg of salt) ÷ (volume of tank).

Steps Building and solving the system click to collapse ▾
1
Model Tank A — what's changing \(x_1\)?

Salt enters Tank A from two sources: pure water (which has zero salt) and the pipe from Tank B. Salt leaves Tank A through the pipe to Tank B at 4 L/min.

Salt in from Tank B: concentration in B is \(\frac{x_2}{50}\) kg/L, flow rate is 1 L/min, so rate in = \(\frac{x_2}{50} \cdot 1\).

Salt out to Tank B: concentration in A is \(\frac{x_1}{50}\) kg/L, flow rate is 4 L/min, so rate out = \(\frac{x_1}{50} \cdot 4\).

\[ x_1' = \underbrace{\frac{x_2}{50} \cdot 1}_{\text{in from B}} - \underbrace{\frac{x_1}{50} \cdot 4}_{\text{out to B}} = \frac{-4}{50}\,x_1 + \frac{1}{50}\,x_2 \]
2
Model Tank B — what's changing \(x_2\)?

Salt enters Tank B only from Tank A at 4 L/min. Salt leaves through the return pipe to A (1 L/min) and the outflow drain (3 L/min) — total out = 4 L/min from B.

Salt in from Tank A: \(\frac{x_1}{50} \cdot 4\).

Salt out (combined): \(\frac{x_2}{50} \cdot (1+3) = \frac{x_2}{50} \cdot 4\).

\[ x_2' = \underbrace{\frac{x_1}{50} \cdot 4}_{\text{in from A}} - \underbrace{\frac{x_2}{50} \cdot 4}_{\text{out}} = \frac{4}{50}\,x_1 - \frac{4}{50}\,x_2 \]
3
Write in matrix form
Collecting the coefficients into a matrix gives us the system \(\mathbf{x}' = A\mathbf{x}\):
\[ A = \begin{bmatrix}-4/50 & 1/50 \\ 4/50 & -4/50\end{bmatrix} \]
We could factor out \(\tfrac{1}{50}\) from \(A\), but that changes the eigenvalues (multiplying \(A\) by a constant scales all eigenvalues by that constant). It's cleaner to keep \(A\) as-is and handle the fractions carefully when computing the determinant.
4
Find the eigenvalues
Setting \(\det(A - \lambda I) = 0\):
\[ \left(\frac{-4}{50} - \lambda\right)^2 - \frac{4}{50}\cdot\frac{1}{50} = 0 \]
Let \(\mu = \frac{-4}{50} - \lambda\). Then \(\mu^2 = \frac{4}{2500}\), so \(\mu = \pm\frac{2}{50}\). This gives:
\[ \lambda_1 = \frac{-4-2}{50} = {\color{#c47b7b}{-\frac{3}{25}}}, \qquad \lambda_2 = \frac{-4+2}{50} = {\color{#c47b7b}{-\frac{1}{25}}} \]
Both eigenvalues are negative. This is the mathematical statement that the salt will eventually die out — both modes of the system decay exponentially. It makes physical sense: we're only pumping in pure water, so the salt must eventually wash out completely.
5
Find the eigenvectors
For \(\lambda_1 = -3/25\), add \(3/25\) to the diagonal and row reduce:
\[ A + \tfrac{3}{25}I = \begin{bmatrix}1/25 & 1/50 \\ 4/50 & 1/25\end{bmatrix} \sim \begin{bmatrix}2 & 1 \\ 0 & 0\end{bmatrix} \;\Rightarrow\; \mathbf{v}_1 = \begin{bmatrix}1 \\ -2\end{bmatrix} \]
For \(\lambda_2 = -1/25\), add \(1/25\) to the diagonal:
\[ A + \tfrac{1}{25}I = \begin{bmatrix}-1/25 & 1/50 \\ 4/50 & -1/25\end{bmatrix} \sim \begin{bmatrix}-2 & 1 \\ 0 & 0\end{bmatrix} \;\Rightarrow\; \mathbf{v}_2 = \begin{bmatrix}1 \\ 2\end{bmatrix} \]
6
Apply initial conditions to find \(c_1\) and \(c_2\)
The general solution is \(\mathbf{x}(t) = c_1 e^{-3t/25}\mathbf{v}_1 + c_2 e^{-t/25}\mathbf{v}_2\). At \(t=0\), both exponentials equal 1, so:
\[ c_1\begin{bmatrix}1\\-2\end{bmatrix} + c_2\begin{bmatrix}1\\2\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}2.5\\0\end{bmatrix} \]
From the second row: \(-2c_1 + 2c_2 = 0\), so \(c_1 = c_2\). From the first row: \(c_1 + c_2 = 2.5\), so \(c_1 = c_2 = 1.25\).
Particular solution (IVP answer)
\[\begin{aligned} x_1(t) &= 1.25\,e^{-3t/25} + 1.25\,e^{-t/25} \\ x_2(t) &= -2.5\,e^{-3t/25} + 2.5\,e^{-t/25} \end{aligned}\]
As \(t \to \infty\), both \(e^{-3t/25}\) and \(e^{-t/25}\) go to zero, so \(x_1, x_2 \to 0\). All the salt eventually gets flushed out by the pure water inflow — exactly what physical intuition says should happen.
Lecture 34 · B9.6
Complex Eigenvalues: Turning \(i\) into \(\cos\) and \(\sin\)

In Lecture 33 we assumed \(A\) had real eigenvalues. But what happens when the characteristic polynomial has no real roots — when the eigenvalues are complex numbers like \(-1 \pm 4i\)? This lecture shows how to handle that case cleanly, and the answer is genuinely satisfying: complex eigenvalues don't give complex solutions. They give oscillating real solutions built from \(\cos\) and \(\sin\).

Part 1 — Setup
Complex eigenvalues always come in conjugate pairs

When the matrix \(A\) has real entries (which it always does in our setting), any nonreal eigenvalue must come paired with its complex conjugate. If \(\alpha + \beta i\) is an eigenvalue, then so is \(\alpha - \beta i\). This is a consequence of the fact that the characteristic polynomial \(\det(A - \lambda I)\) has real coefficients — complex roots of real polynomials always come in conjugate pairs.

We write these eigenvalues as \(\alpha \pm \beta i\), where \(\alpha\) is the real part and \(\beta \neq 0\) is the imaginary part. The associated eigenvector for \(\alpha + \beta i\) will itself be a complex vector — we write it as \(\mathbf{a} + \mathbf{b}i\), where \(\mathbf{a}\) and \(\mathbf{b}\) are ordinary real vectors.

⚠️
Notation warning: Be careful not to confuse \(\alpha, \beta\) (parts of the eigenvalue) with \(\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b}\) (parts of the eigenvector). They look similar and it's a very easy mix-up to make, especially under pressure on an exam.
Part 2 — The Complex Solution
The same formula still works — but gives a complex-valued answer

From Lecture 33, we know that for a real eigenvalue \(\lambda\) with eigenvector \(\mathbf{v}\), the function \(e^{\lambda t}\mathbf{v}\) is a solution. The exact same logic applies when \(\lambda\) is complex. If \(\alpha + \beta i\) is an eigenvalue with eigenvector \(\mathbf{a} + \mathbf{b}i\), then:

The complex solution \(w(t)\)
\[ w(t) = e^{(\alpha + \beta i)t}(\mathbf{a} + \mathbf{b}i) \]

This satisfies \(w'(t) = Aw(t)\) for the same reason as before: differentiating gives \((\alpha + \beta i)e^{(\alpha+\beta i)t}(\mathbf{a}+\mathbf{b}i)\), and since \(\mathbf{a}+\mathbf{b}i\) is an eigenvector, applying \(A\) to it gives the same thing. The verification is identical to the real case.

But there's a catch: \(w(t)\) is a complex-valued vector function. We're solving a real physical system, so we want real solutions. The question is: how do we extract real solutions from this complex one?

Part 3 — The Key Insight
Split \(w(t)\) into its real and imaginary parts using Euler's identity

The trick is Euler's identity: \(e^{i\theta} = \cos\theta + i\sin\theta\). We use it to separate \(e^{(\alpha+\beta i)t}\) into a real exponential and a complex oscillating factor:

Expanding \(w(t)\) step by step
\[\begin{aligned} w(t) &= e^{(\alpha + \beta i)t}(\mathbf{a} + \mathbf{b}i) \\[8pt] &= e^{\alpha t} \cdot e^{i\beta t} \cdot (\mathbf{a} + \mathbf{b}i) \\[8pt] &= e^{\alpha t}[\cos(\beta t) + i\sin(\beta t)](\mathbf{a} + \mathbf{b}i) \end{aligned}\]

Now we multiply out the bracket \([\cos(\beta t) + i\sin(\beta t)](\mathbf{a} + \mathbf{b}i)\). This is just complex number multiplication — distribute everything and remember that \(i^2 = -1\):

Multiplying out — collecting real and imaginary parts
\[\begin{aligned} &[\cos(\beta t) + i\sin(\beta t)](\mathbf{a} + \mathbf{b}i) \\[6pt] &= \cos(\beta t)\mathbf{a} + i\cos(\beta t)\mathbf{b} + i\sin(\beta t)\mathbf{a} + i^2\sin(\beta t)\mathbf{b} \\[6pt] &= \underbrace{\bigl[\cos(\beta t)\,\mathbf{a} - \sin(\beta t)\,\mathbf{b}\bigr]}_{\text{real part}} \;+\; i\underbrace{\bigl[\sin(\beta t)\,\mathbf{a} + \cos(\beta t)\,\mathbf{b}\bigr]}_{\text{imaginary part}} \end{aligned}\]

So \(w(t) = e^{\alpha t}[\text{real part}] + i \cdot e^{\alpha t}[\text{imaginary part}]\). We have successfully split the complex solution into its real and imaginary components, which we name \(w_1(t)\) and \(w_2(t)\).

Real part → \(w_1(t)\)
\[ w_1(t) = e^{\alpha t}\bigl[\cos(\beta t)\,\mathbf{a} - \sin(\beta t)\,\mathbf{b}\bigr] \]

The \(-\) sign between the two terms is fixed. This is the real part of \(w(t)\).

Imaginary part → \(w_2(t)\)
\[ w_2(t) = e^{\alpha t}\bigl[\sin(\beta t)\,\mathbf{a} + \cos(\beta t)\,\mathbf{b}\bigr] \]

The \(+\) sign between the two terms is fixed. This is the imaginary part of \(w(t)\).

Part 4 — Why This Works
Why are the real and imaginary parts each a valid solution?

This is the crucial step. We have \(w(t) = w_1(t) + i\,w_2(t)\) where \(w_1, w_2\) are real-valued vector functions. We've shown \(w'(t) = Aw(t)\), which means:

\[ w_1'(t) + i\,w_2'(t) = A\bigl[w_1(t) + i\,w_2(t)\bigr] = Aw_1(t) + i\,Aw_2(t) \]

Since \(A\) is a real matrix, we can equate the real parts on both sides and the imaginary parts on both sides separately. Real parts give \(w_1' = Aw_1\) and imaginary parts give \(w_2' = Aw_2\). Both \(w_1\) and \(w_2\) individually satisfy the system — and they're both completely real.

✓
The punchline: One complex eigenvalue \(\alpha + \beta i\) (with its conjugate \(\alpha - \beta i\)) yields two real solutions \(w_1(t)\) and \(w_2(t)\). The conjugate pair accounts for exactly two solutions — which is the right count, since a conjugate pair represents two eigenvalues.
Why not do the same thing for the conjugate eigenvalue \(\alpha - \beta i\)?

You might wonder: shouldn't the conjugate eigenvalue \(\alpha - \beta i\) give us two more solutions? It turns out it gives exactly the same \(w_1\) and \(w_2\) we already have — nothing new. Here's why: the conjugate eigenvalue has eigenvector \(\mathbf{a} - \mathbf{b}i\). When you work through the same expansion, cosine is an even function (\(\cos(-\beta t) = \cos(\beta t)\)) and sine is odd (\(\sin(-\beta t) = -\sin(\beta t)\)). So the real part stays the same as \(w_1\), and the imaginary part becomes \(-w_2\) — which is just a constant multiple of \(w_2\) and counts as the same solution. The conjugate pair together gives exactly two solutions, as expected.

Signs are not flexible. The formulas for \(w_1\) and \(w_2\) have specific signs: \(w_1\) has a minus between the \(\mathbf{a}\) and \(\mathbf{b}\) terms, while \(w_2\) has a plus. These are not interchangeable — \(w_1\) is the real part of \(w(t)\) and \(w_2\) is the imaginary part. Swapping signs would give something that is neither \(w_1\) nor a constant multiple of it, and would no longer be a solution. This is a common exam mistake.

Worked Example
Solving a system with complex eigenvalues

Let's work through a complete example. Consider the system:

The system
\[\begin{aligned} x_1' &= -x_1 - 2x_2 \\ x_2' &= \phantom{-}8x_1 - x_2 \end{aligned}\]
Steps Full solution walkthrough click to collapse ▾
1
Write the coefficient matrix \(A\)
Read the coefficients row by row as before.
\[ A = \begin{bmatrix}-1 & -2 \\ 8 & -1\end{bmatrix} \]
2
Find the eigenvalues
Subtract \(\lambda\) from the diagonal and set the determinant to zero. Notice the diagonal entries are both \(-1\), so \(A - \lambda I\) has \(-1-\lambda\) in both diagonal slots:
\[ \det(A - \lambda I) = (-1-\lambda)^2 - (-2)(8) = (-1-\lambda)^2 + 16 \overset{?}{=} 0 \]
This means \((-1-\lambda)^2 = -16\), so \(-1-\lambda = \pm 4i\), giving:
\[ \lambda = -1 \pm 4i \quad\Rightarrow\quad \alpha = -1,\quad \beta = 4 \]
The real part \(\alpha = -1\) tells us solutions will decay like \(e^{-t}\) — they shrink over time. The imaginary part \(\beta = 4\) tells us solutions will oscillate with angular frequency 4 — they spin around. Together, the system spirals inward.
3
Find the eigenvector for \(\lambda = -1 + 4i\) only
Because the conjugate gives nothing new, we only work with one eigenvalue. Plugging \(\lambda = -1 + 4i\) into \(A - \lambda I\):
\[ A - (-1+4i)I = \begin{bmatrix}-1-(-1+4i) & -2 \\ 8 & -1-(-1+4i)\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}-4i & -2 \\ 8 & -4i\end{bmatrix} \]
Since this is an eigenvalue, the two rows are dependent — we only need one equation. Using the first row: \(-4i\,y_1 - 2y_2 = 0\). Let's pick the simplest choice, \(y_1 = -1\), and solve for \(y_2\):
\[ -4i(-1) - 2y_2 = 0 \quad\Rightarrow\quad 4i = 2y_2 \quad\Rightarrow\quad y_2 = 2i \]
So the (complex) eigenvector is \(\begin{bmatrix}-1 \\ 2i\end{bmatrix}\). Now we read off the real and imaginary parts:
\[ \begin{bmatrix}-1 \\ 2i\end{bmatrix} = \underbrace{\begin{bmatrix}-1 \\ 0\end{bmatrix}}_{\mathbf{a}\text{ (real part)}} + \underbrace{\begin{bmatrix}0 \\ 2\end{bmatrix}}_{\mathbf{b}\text{ (imaginary part)}}i \]
Note: we could also have chosen \(y_1 = i\), which would give a different (but equally valid) eigenvector — any nonzero scalar multiple, real or complex, is acceptable.
4
Plug into the \(w_1, w_2\) formulas
We have \(\alpha = -1\), \(\beta = 4\), \(\mathbf{a} = \begin{bmatrix}-1\\0\end{bmatrix}\), \(\mathbf{b} = \begin{bmatrix}0\\2\end{bmatrix}\). Substitute directly:
\[ w_1(t) = e^{-t}\left[\cos(4t)\begin{bmatrix}-1\\0\end{bmatrix} - \sin(4t)\begin{bmatrix}0\\2\end{bmatrix}\right] = e^{-t}\begin{bmatrix}-\cos(4t) \\ -2\sin(4t)\end{bmatrix} \]
\[ w_2(t) = e^{-t}\left[\sin(4t)\begin{bmatrix}-1\\0\end{bmatrix} + \cos(4t)\begin{bmatrix}0\\2\end{bmatrix}\right] = e^{-t}\begin{bmatrix}-\sin(4t) \\ 2\cos(4t)\end{bmatrix} \]
Notice how \(e^{-t}\) factors out in front of everything. This is the decay envelope from \(\alpha = -1\). The vectors inside oscillate between the \(\mathbf{a}\) and \(\mathbf{b}\) directions — that's the rotation from \(\beta = 4\).
5
Write the general solution
The general solution is \(c_1 w_1(t) + c_2 w_2(t)\). Writing out the scalar components explicitly:
General solution
\[ \mathbf{x}(t) = c_1 e^{-t}\begin{bmatrix}-\cos(4t) \\ -2\sin(4t)\end{bmatrix} + c_2 e^{-t}\begin{bmatrix}-\sin(4t) \\ 2\cos(4t)\end{bmatrix} \]
Component form: \(x_1(t) = -c_1 e^{-t}\cos(4t) - c_2 e^{-t}\sin(4t)\) and \(x_2(t) = -2c_1 e^{-t}\sin(4t) + 2c_2 e^{-t}\cos(4t)\). As \(t \to \infty\), the \(e^{-t}\) factor drives everything to zero — the spiral decays inward.
Part 5 — Bigger Picture
What about larger systems?

The procedure above works for any size matrix. If \(A\) is \(3 \times 3\) with one real eigenvalue and one conjugate pair, you handle each type separately and combine: the real eigenvalue gives one \(e^{\lambda t}\mathbf{v}\) term (as in Lec 33), and the complex conjugate pair gives \(w_1\) and \(w_2\) as above. Together that's three terms, which is exactly right for a third-order system.

For a \(10 \times 10\) matrix the calculation is much longer, but the fundamental idea doesn't change. The only thing that can still cause problems — regardless of matrix size — is repeated eigenvalues (whether real or complex) that don't have enough independent eigenvectors. That's the topic of the Addendum tab: why the "\(t\) trick" is not just a lucky guess, but falls out naturally from variation of parameters.

Real eigenvalue \(\lambda\)
Gives one solution: \(e^{\lambda t}\mathbf{v}\). If \(\lambda < 0\), the solution decays. If \(\lambda > 0\), it grows. If \(\lambda = 0\), it's constant.
Complex pair \(\alpha \pm \beta i\)
Gives two solutions: \(w_1(t)\) and \(w_2(t)\). The \(e^{\alpha t}\) factor controls growth or decay; the \(\cos(\beta t)\), \(\sin(\beta t)\) factors produce oscillation.
Addendum — Why Multiplying by \(t\) Works

Three situations use the "multiply by \(t\)" fix. This addendum proves why it works using VOP — a deeper justification than just "brute-force checking it".

The Three Situations Where You Multiply by \(t\)

(1) Repeated real root in homogeneous ODE
Root \(r\) repeated → \(e^{rt}\) and \(te^{rt}\). The \(te^{rt}\) is derived, not guessed.
(2) MUC overlap with homogeneous solution
When the "natural guess" for \(y_p\) is already in \(y_c\), multiply by \(t\) (or \(t^2\) for double overlap).
(3) Repeated eigenvalue in a system
Repeated eigenvalue with insufficient eigenvectors → generalized eigenvector approach also uses \(t\) multiplication.
Common Root
All three cases involve a function that "lives in the kernel" — multiplying by \(t\) moves it out of the null space (the homogeneous solution space). This is a linear algebra idea.
Addendum · VOP Derivation Proving \(te^{rt}\) is the second solution when \(r\) is a repeated root Why \(t\) works ▾
1
Setup — one known solution, find a second
In \(ay'' + by' + cy = 0\), we know \({\color{#e8b86d}{y_1 = e^{rt}}}\) where \(r\) is a repeated root. Try a second solution of the form \(y = u_1(t)\cdot y_1(t)\):
\[\begin{aligned} y &= {\color{#b48fd4}{u_1}}{\color{#e8b86d}{e^{rt}}}\\ y' &= {\color{#b48fd4}{u_1'}}{\color{#e8b86d}{e^{rt}}} + {\color{#b48fd4}{u_1}}\cdot r{\color{#e8b86d}{e^{rt}}}\\ y'' &= {\color{#b48fd4}{u_1''}}{\color{#e8b86d}{e^{rt}}} + 2{\color{#b48fd4}{u_1'}} r{\color{#e8b86d}{e^{rt}}} + {\color{#b48fd4}{u_1}} r^2{\color{#e8b86d}{e^{rt}}} \end{aligned}\]
2
Substitute into the ODE
\[ a({\color{#b48fd4}{u_1''}} + 2{\color{#b48fd4}{u_1'}} r + {\color{#b48fd4}{u_1}} r^2){\color{#e8b86d}{e^{rt}}} + b({\color{#b48fd4}{u_1'}} + {\color{#b48fd4}{u_1}} r){\color{#e8b86d}{e^{rt}}} + c{\color{#b48fd4}{u_1}}{\color{#e8b86d}{e^{rt}}} = 0 \]
Group by \(u_1''\), \(u_1'\), and \(u_1\) — the \(u_1\) terms form exactly \({\color{#e8b86d}{a(r^2)+b(r)+c}}\) times \(u_1\), which is zero since \(r\) is a root of the characteristic polynomial:
\[ a{\color{#b48fd4}{u_1''}}{\color{#e8b86d}{e^{rt}}} + {\color{#b48fd4}{u_1'}}{\color{#e8b86d}{e^{rt}}}(2ar+b) = 0 \]
3
The repeated-root condition kills the \(u_1'\) term too
Since \(r\) is a repeated root of \(ax^2+bx+c\), it is also a root of the derivative \(2ax+b\), so \(2ar+b = 0\):
\[ a{\color{#b48fd4}{u_1''}}{\color{#e8b86d}{e^{rt}}} = 0 \;\Rightarrow\; {\color{#b48fd4}{u_1''}} = 0 \]
4
Integrate twice — the \(t\) emerges naturally
\[ {\color{#b48fd4}{u_1''}} = 0 \;\Rightarrow\; {\color{#b48fd4}{u_1'}} = F \;\Rightarrow\; {\color{#b48fd4}{u_1}} = Ft + G \]
\[ y = (Ft+G){\color{#e8b86d}{e^{rt}}} = F\cdot{\color{#7eb8a4}{te^{rt}}} + G\cdot{\color{#e8b86d}{e^{rt}}} \]
The \(G e^{rt}\) part is just \(y_1\) again. The genuinely new solution is \({\color{#7eb8a4}{te^{rt}}}\). This is why multiplying by \(t\) works — it's not a guess, it falls directly out of VOP.
This derivation is why the method generalizes: for a 10th-order equation with \(r\) as a repeated root, the same VOP argument produces \(te^{rt}, t^2 e^{rt}, \ldots\) as the additional solutions. Brute-force checking would be impractical at that scale.
Conclusion
When \(r\) is a repeated real root, the second linearly independent solution is \(te^{rt}\).
It emerges from VOP: \(u_1''=0 \Rightarrow u_1 = Ft+G\), so \(y = (Ft+G)e^{rt}\).

\[ \det(A-\lambda I) = (1-\lambda)^2 + 8 = 0 \;\Rightarrow\; (1-\lambda)^2 = -8 \;\Rightarrow\; {\color{#c47b7b}{\lambda = 1 \pm 2\sqrt{2}\,i}} \]
\({\color{#c47b7b}{\alpha = 1}},\; {\color{#c47b7b}{\beta = 2\sqrt{2}}}\). Since \(\alpha > 0\), solutions grow over time.
2
Eigenvector for \(\lambda = 1+2\sqrt{2}\,i\)
\[ A - (1+2\sqrt{2}\,i)I = \begin{bmatrix}-2\sqrt{2}\,i & -2 \\ 4 & -2\sqrt{2}\,i\end{bmatrix} \]
From first row: \(-2\sqrt{2}\,i\,y_1 - 2y_2 = 0\). Pick \(y_1 = 1\) → \(y_2 = -\sqrt{2}\,i\).
\[ \text{Eigenvector: }\begin{bmatrix}1\\-\sqrt{2}\,i\end{bmatrix} = \underbrace{\begin{bmatrix}1\\0\end{bmatrix}}_{{\color{#e8b86d}{\mathbf{a}}}} + \underbrace{\begin{bmatrix}0\\-\sqrt{2}\end{bmatrix}}_{{\color{#7eb8a4}{\mathbf{b}}}}i \]
3
Apply \(w_1, w_2\) with \(e^{\alpha t} = e^t\)
\[ {\color{#e8b86d}{w_1}} = e^{{\color{#c47b7b}{t}}}\!\left[\cos({\color{#c47b7b}{2\sqrt{2}}}t)\begin{bmatrix}{\color{#e8b86d}{1}}\\{\color{#e8b86d}{0}}\end{bmatrix} - \sin({\color{#c47b7b}{2\sqrt{2}}}t)\begin{bmatrix}{\color{#7eb8a4}{0}}\\{\color{#7eb8a4}{-\sqrt{2}}}\end{bmatrix}\right] = e^t\begin{bmatrix}\cos(2\sqrt{2}\,t) \\ \sqrt{2}\sin(2\sqrt{2}\,t)\end{bmatrix} \]
\[ {\color{#7eb8a4}{w_2}} = e^{{\color{#c47b7b}{t}}}\!\left[\sin({\color{#c47b7b}{2\sqrt{2}}}t)\begin{bmatrix}{\color{#e8b86d}{1}}\\{\color{#e8b86d}{0}}\end{bmatrix} + \cos({\color{#c47b7b}{2\sqrt{2}}}t)\begin{bmatrix}{\color{#7eb8a4}{0}}\\{\color{#7eb8a4}{-\sqrt{2}}}\end{bmatrix}\right] = e^t\begin{bmatrix}\sin(2\sqrt{2}\,t) \\ -\sqrt{2}\cos(2\sqrt{2}\,t)\end{bmatrix} \]
General solution
\[ \mathbf{x}(t) = c_1\, e^t\begin{bmatrix}\cos(2\sqrt{2}\,t) \\ \sqrt{2}\sin(2\sqrt{2}\,t)\end{bmatrix} + c_2\, e^t\begin{bmatrix}\sin(2\sqrt{2}\,t) \\ -\sqrt{2}\cos(2\sqrt{2}\,t)\end{bmatrix} \]
Since \(\alpha = 1 > 0\), both components grow exponentially while oscillating — an unstable spiral.
